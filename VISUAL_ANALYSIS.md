# VISUAL_ANALYSIS.md

This document interprets reference sources for Locker. It does not define brand identity; `BRAND_DNA.md` does.

## Primary Visual Reference

### ApeChain

Reference confidence: reviewed as a visual and structural reference.

Why it is useful:

ApeChain shows how a digital ecosystem can feel bold, cultural, and category-led without becoming a plain catalog. Its app-directory structure maps well to Locker's long-term expansion across digital goods categories.

What should be borrowed:

- Category-led browsing.
- Strong product or app spotlight moments.
- Confident card composition.
- A sense of digital ecosystem rather than one-off store.
- Bold hierarchy with clear entry points.

What should never be copied:

- Web3 language and crypto-coded identity.
- Meme or IP-dependent visual culture.
- Overly loud cultural maximalism.
- Any speculative or chain/coin symbolism.

## UX References

### LIS-SKINS

Reference confidence: reviewed as a UX and Steam-market behavior reference.

Why it is useful:

LIS-SKINS reflects familiar Steam-market behavior: Steam login, currency controls, trade-related requirements, and fast transaction expectations.

What should be borrowed:

- Steam-first requirement visibility.
- Practical FAQ and help orientation.
- Clear user prerequisites before transaction.
- Familiar market-language patterns where they reduce confusion.

What should never be copied:

- Commodity reseller feel.
- Visual clutter.
- "Sell for real money" framing if Locker's launch is buy-first.

### GGSEL

Reference confidence: reviewed as a UX and broader digital-goods marketplace reference.

Why it is useful:

GGSEL shows how non-gaming digital goods are categorized, sold, reviewed, and supported. It is relevant if Locker includes subscriptions or broader digital products.

What should be borrowed:

- Clear category structure for non-skin products.
- Buyer trust patterns.
- Legal, support, and refund visibility.
- Review and social-proof expectations, if verified and authentic.

What should never be copied:

- Broad reseller clutter.
- Mixed seller-quality signals.
- Marketplace randomness that weakens Locker's account-centered identity.

## Visual System Conclusions

Locker should use a restrained, premium digital system that lets product imagery, price, status, and account context carry the experience. It should borrow ecosystem confidence from ApeChain and art-direction quality from 1UP Nova while avoiding crypto, casino, and generic gaming signals.

## Motion Conclusions

Motion should communicate state and confidence. It should help users understand access, progress, confirmation, delivery, and resolution. It should never create suspense around spending money.

## Composition Conclusions

Composition should balance brand calm with marketplace efficiency. Brand surfaces can be spacious and atmospheric. Catalog and checkout surfaces should be structured, scannable, and accountable.

## Typography Conclusions

Typography should feel modern, precise, and durable. It should support product names, prices, statuses, and legal/support clarity. Avoid novelty gamer type and over-stylized luxury typography.

## Trust-Building Conclusions

Trust should come from visible balance logic, clear requirements, readable product information, explicit order states, legal clarity, and support access. Reference sites offer useful patterns, but Locker should feel more coherent and more professionally operated than a typical reseller marketplace.
